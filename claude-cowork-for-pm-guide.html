Project managers live and die by their ability to synthesize information, communicate clearly, and anticipate what comes next. Chapter 00 — Before You Start

Setting Up Claude Cowork

Required: Claude Pro, Max, Team, or Enterprise plan · Desktop app · macOS (Windows coming)

Claude Cowork is built into the Claude desktop app — not the browser. Unlike regular Claude chat, Cowork can access your local files, run multi-step tasks autonomously, connect to tools like Google Drive and Slack, and even control your browser via Claude in Chrome. Here's how to get from zero to your first real PM task.

Step-by-Step Installation

1

Download the Claude Desktop App

Go to claude.com/download and download the installer for your OS. macOS uses a standard .dmg; Windows uses an MSIX installer. Run it and launch Claude from your Applications folder (Mac) or Start menu (Windows).

macOS is fully supported · Windows: Cowork coming soon
2

Sign In With Your Paid Account

Cowork requires a paid plan — Pro, Max, Team, or Enterprise. Free accounts can join a waitlist. Sign in with your Anthropic account credentials. Your conversations, memory, and preferences sync across web, desktop, and mobile.

3

Click the Cowork Tab

At the top of the desktop app you'll see three tabs: Chat, Cowork, and Code. Click Cowork. This is a separate mode — not just a chat window. Claude here can plan multi-step tasks, access your files, and execute without you prompting each step.

Key difference: Chat responds. Cowork executes.
4

Grant Folder Permissions

For any task involving local files, you'll need to point Claude at a folder. It asks for permission before accessing anything. Start by granting access to a project folder — e.g., your active project's documents directory. Claude reads what's there and can create, edit, rename, and sort files within it.

5

Set Global Instructions

Go to Settings → Cowork → Edit Global Instructions. This is where you tell Claude standing context about your role. For a PM, include: your job title, the industry you work in, your preferred output format (e.g., structured docs vs. bullet summaries), and any terminology specific to your team.

Example: "I'm a project manager at a fintech firm. I communicate with both technical teams and senior executives. Outputs should be clear, structured, and jargon-light unless context requires otherwise."
6

Connect Your Tools (Optional but Powerful)

Cowork supports web-based connectors including Google Drive, Slack, and others. Go to Settings → Connectors and enable what you use. You can also enable Claude in Chrome — this lets Cowork navigate your browser, fill forms, and pull information from web tools directly, without you switching windows.

7

Run Your First Task

Type your first real task in plain language — not a prompt, a goal. Example: "Read the five documents in my /Projects/Q2-Launch folder and produce a one-page project brief summarizing scope, stakeholders, and open risks." Claude shows you its plan, waits for approval, then executes. You review the output.

Cowork shows its plan before acting. You approve before anything changes.

Cowork vs. Regular Claude Chat — Key Differences

Regular Chat
Responds to Prompts

Answers questions, drafts content, analyses text you paste. Cannot touch your file system or apps.

Claude Cowork
Executes Tasks

Reads, creates, and edits local files. Runs multi-step workflows. Can control your browser via Claude in Chrome.

Key PM Use
Outcome-Oriented

Describe a finished deliverable. Cowork figures out the steps and produces it — status report, risk register, sorted folder.

Important Safety Note

Cowork has real file access. It shows you its plan before executing and asks permission at each consequential step — but grant folder access carefully. Start with a dedicated project folder, not your entire drive. You can delete any Cowork task from the sidebar at any time.

PM Quick Win — First Task to Try

Drop three to five meeting notes, emails, or briefs into a folder. Point Cowork at it and say: "Read these documents and produce a project initiation summary: goals, stakeholders, open questions, and a suggested next step." You'll have a usable first draft in under two minutes.

Before Phase 01 — Quick Start

Your First Week With Cowork

Before diving into the full playbook, here's the fastest path from installation to real, recurring value. These four tasks, in this order, will show you what Cowork can actually do — without overwhelming you with every feature at once.

Day 1 · Setup
Configure Global Instructions

Go to Settings → Cowork → Edit Global Instructions. Write three sentences: your role, your industry, and your preferred output format. Everything Cowork produces from here will be calibrated to you.

Day 2 · First Real Task
Meeting Minutes from a Transcript

Drop a recent meeting transcript into a folder. Point Cowork at it and ask for decisions, action items, and a one-paragraph summary. This shows you the file-access workflow with zero risk.

Day 3–4 · First Doc Generation
Status Report from Raw Notes

Keep a running notes file this week. On Friday, point Cowork at it and ask for an executive-ready status report. By Monday you'll have a repeatable weekly workflow.

Day 5 · Automate It
Schedule Your First Recurring Task

Type /schedule in any Cowork task. Set the status report to run every Friday at 4pm. You've just automated your most time-consuming weekly deliverable.

The Rule of Week One

Don't try to use every chapter of this guide immediately. Pick one repeating PM pain point — status reports, meeting minutes, risk logs — automate it completely in week one, then expand. Depth beats breadth when building a new workflow.

Phase 01 — Initiation

Kicking Off the Project

The initiation phase is where ambiguity is highest and mistakes are most expensive. Cowork helps you structure the chaos before a single sprint is planned — and because it can read your existing files directly, you skip the copy-paste step entirely.

1.1 Project Charter from Raw Files

Point Cowork at a folder of kick-off notes, emails, and pre-read documents. Describe what you need as an outcome, not a prompt.

Cowork Task — Project Charter

Read all documents in [folder path]. Based on what's there, draft a project charter as a formatted Word document covering: (1) one-paragraph purpose statement, (2) three to five measurable objectives, (3) explicit in-scope and out-of-scope items, (4) key stakeholders with their roles, (5) top three assumptions and constraints, (6) success criteria evaluable at project close. Save it as ProjectCharter_v1.docx in the same folder.

1.2 Stakeholder Analysis

Methodology: Both Agile Waterfall
Stakeholder Profiles Both

Paste a list and ask Cowork to generate a profile for each: role, likely concerns, communication preference, and disengagement risk — saved as a table in a document.

Power / Interest Matrix Both

Give Cowork your list and ask it to map each person into a 2×2 grid, then generate engagement strategies per quadrant — as a formatted output file.

Conflict Pre-mortem Both

"Given these stakeholders and project goals, where are the most likely conflicts and how do I get ahead of them?" Ask Cowork to read the brief first.

RACI Matrix Waterfall

List your workstreams and team members. Cowork produces a formatted RACI table. Use it as a conversation starter, not a final answer.

Pro Tip

Never treat Cowork's stakeholder analysis as complete. It doesn't know the personal dynamics, political history, or unspoken tensions in your organization. Use it to surface structure — you supply the context.

Phase 02 — Planning

Building the Plan That Survives Contact

Planning is where most projects are won or lost. Cowork can read your existing docs and produce planning artifacts directly — no copy-pasting into prompts.

2.1 Work Breakdown Structure

Cowork Task — WBS

Read the project charter in [folder]. Create a three-level WBS — Level 1 is the project, Level 2 is major phases, Level 3 is key deliverables per phase. Then identify three to five commonly overlooked work packages for this type of project. Save as WBS_v1.xlsx with a structured table format.

2.2 Risk Register

Step 1
Seed with project context
Point Cowork at your project folder. It reads the charter, scope docs, and any briefs already there.
Step 2
Generate 15–20 risks across categories
Ask for technical, organizational, external, resource, and schedule risks — each with a one-line description.
Step 3
Score and prioritize
Rate each risk by likelihood (1–5) and impact (1–5). Ask Cowork to sort and produce a risk heatmap summary.
Step 4
Generate mitigation strategies
For the top 5 risks: specific mitigation actions, contingency plans, and suggested risk owners.
Step 5
Ask the uncomfortable question
"What's the one risk on this project that teams typically dismiss but shouldn't?" This often surfaces the real blind spot.

2.3 Milestone & Schedule Planning

Dependency Mapping Waterfall

List your deliverables and ask Cowork to identify finish-to-start and start-to-start dependencies, output as a structured table.

Buffer Estimation Both

"Given these tasks and a team of [X], where should I place schedule buffers and how large, using a critical chain approach?"

Sprint Allocation Agile

Paste your backlog and team velocity. Ask Cowork to suggest a sprint plan accounting for dependencies and capacity, saved to a file.

Deadline Stress Test Both

"If this deadline is fixed and scope is [X], what are the three most realistic ways this project fails to deliver on time?"

2.4 Budget Planning

Cowork Task — Budget Framework

I'm planning a [project type] with a team of [N people] over [X months]. Generate a budget category framework in Excel covering labor, tools/licenses, infrastructure, change management, training, and contingency. For each row, include typical sub-items and flag the most common underestimation mistakes in a notes column. Save as BudgetFramework_v1.xlsx.

Watch Out

Cowork doesn't know your organization's actual rates or vendor contracts. Budget structures it produces are scaffolding, not estimates. Always cross-reference with historical actuals and procurement.

Phase 03 — Requirements

Turning Vague Into Verifiable

Requirements are where projects slip most silently. Cowork can read existing documentation, flag gaps, and produce workshop materials directly — without you assembling them manually.

3.1 Elicitation Workshop Prep

Cowork Task — Workshop Prep

Read the project brief in [folder]. Generate a requirements elicitation workshop pack: (a) 20 questions organized by theme — business goals, user needs, constraints, edge cases; (b) a workshop agenda with suggested time blocks; (c) a blank requirements capture template. Save as WorkshopPack_v1.docx.

3.2 User Story Writing

01

Paste or point at the feature request

Give Cowork the raw request verbatim — or point it at a folder of stakeholder notes. Don't clean the input first.

02

Request story + acceptance criteria

"As a [user], I want [action], so that [outcome]" with 3–5 Gherkin-style acceptance criteria per story.

03

Ask for edge cases

"What edge cases or negative scenarios should this story also account for?"

04

Validate against INVEST criteria

"Does this story meet the INVEST criteria? If not, how should I split or rewrite it?"

3.3 Requirements Quality Audit

Cowork Task — Quality Audit

Read the requirements document in [folder]. Flag any requirements that are: (a) untestable as written, (b) lacking clear business justification, (c) conflicting with another requirement, or (d) solutions disguised as requirements. For each flag, suggest how to fix it. Output as an annotated version of the original document.

Interlude — Prompt Craft

Writing Cowork Tasks That Actually Deliver

There's a significant difference between a Cowork task that produces a usable output on the first try and one that generates three rounds of correction. The difference isn't intelligence — it's instruction quality. Here's how to write tasks that land.

The Four Elements of a Good Cowork Task

01
Describe the outcome, not the process

Tell Cowork what the finished deliverable looks like, not what steps to follow. It figures out the path — you define the destination.

02
Specify the output format explicitly

Say "save as a Word document with a summary table at the top" rather than leaving format open. Ambiguous format requests produce ambiguous outputs.

03
Name the audience

"Write this for a non-technical steering committee" changes tone, vocabulary, and structure more than any other instruction. Always include it.

04
Constrain the scope

Cowork will expand to fill undefined space. If you want a one-page summary, say one page. If you want five risks, say five. Constraints produce tighter outputs.

Weak Task vs. Strong Task — Side by Side

Weak Task

Summarize the project status.

Strong Task

Read weekly-notes.txt in my project folder. Write a 250-word status update for a non-technical steering committee covering RAG status, top three risks, and one decision needed. Save as StatusReport_Apr25.docx.

Weak Task

Make a risk register for this project.

Strong Task

Read all documents in /Q2-Launch/. Identify 15 risks across technical, organizational, and schedule categories. Rate each by likelihood and impact (1–5). Sort by risk score. Save as RiskRegister_v1.xlsx with one risk per row.

Weak Task

Write a lessons learned report.

Strong Task

Read all documents in /ProjectAlpha/. Produce a lessons learned report structured by phase: Initiation, Planning, Execution, Closeout. For each phase: what worked, what failed, root cause, and one recommendation. Max two pages. Save as LessonsLearned_AlphaFinal.docx.

The One-Sentence Test

Before sending any Cowork task, read it aloud as if briefing a new analyst. If it leaves obvious questions unanswered — "what format?", "how long?", "for who?" — answer those before you send it. That's the whole test.

Phase 04 — Communication

Saying the Right Thing to the Right Person

Communication is a PM's most time-consuming responsibility. Cowork can read your raw notes, meeting transcripts, or log files and produce polished, audience-ready outputs directly.

4.1 Status Reports from Raw Notes

Cowork Task — Weekly Status Report

Read the file [weekly-notes.txt] in my project folder. Write a status report for an executive audience — max 300 words — covering: RAG status, key accomplishments this week, top three risks or issues, next week's priorities, and decisions needed from leadership. Save as StatusReport_[date].docx.

Audience-Tailored Updates

Ask Cowork to rewrite the same update for three audiences — steering committee, delivery team, external vendor — as three separate files, each calibrated in tone and detail level.

Delay Notifications

Describe the situation. Cowork drafts a delay notification that's honest, specific about impact, and proposes a path forward — without sounding defensive.

Escalation Memos

Give Cowork the issue, history, and what you need from leadership. It drafts a memo with the ask in the first sentence, saved and ready to send.

Meeting Agendas

Describe the meeting purpose and attendees. Cowork generates a timed agenda with pre-reads, discussion questions, and decision points — saved as a shareable doc.

4.2 Meeting Minutes from Transcripts

This is one of Cowork's clearest time-savers. Drop a transcript file into your project folder and give it one instruction.

Cowork Task — Meeting Minutes

Read the meeting transcript [transcript.txt] in my folder. Extract and save as MeetingMinutes_[date].docx: (1) decisions made, (2) action items with owner and due date in a table, (3) open issues needing follow-up, (4) a one-paragraph summary for the project log.

4.3 Difficult Conversations

Scope Pushback Scripts

Describe the scope creep situation. Cowork drafts talking points for saying no professionally — with alternative offers that preserve the relationship.

Performance Conversations

Give Cowork the behavior, impact, and desired change. It drafts the structure — you add the human judgment before delivering it.

Vendor Negotiation Prep

Describe the situation. Ask for your leverage points, their likely objections, and suggested counter-positions per issue.

Sponsor Re-alignment

When priorities shift mid-project, ask Cowork to draft a re-alignment memo that reframes without invalidating earlier decisions.

Cowork is not a communications department. It's an execution layer. The most effective PMs use it to eliminate mechanical assembly — then apply the judgment the tool doesn't have.

Phase 05 — Execution & Monitoring

Keeping the Machine Running

During execution, your job shifts to pattern recognition and fast intervention. Cowork can read across your project files and produce monitoring outputs — issue logs, EV narratives, retro packs — without manual assembly.

5.1 Change Request Analysis

Cowork Task — Change Request

Read the change request in [CR-001.docx] and the current project plan in [folder]. Analyze: (1) what's actually being asked vs. what's stated, (2) impact on scope, timeline, and budget, (3) dependencies affected, (4) three options — approve, reject, partial — with trade-offs for each. Save as CR-001-Analysis.docx.

5.2 Earned Value Narrative

Cowork Task — EV to Plain English

EV metrics this period: PV = [X], EV = [Y], AC = [Z], SPI = [n], CPI = [n]. Write a one-paragraph plain-English narrative for my steering committee: what these numbers mean, whether we're in trouble, and what corrective action is underway. Append to the current status report file.

5.3 Retrospectives & Monitoring

Retro Question Design

Describe what went wrong this sprint. Ask Cowork for retro prompts that surface root causes rather than symptoms — saved as a facilitation guide.

Retro Output Synthesis

Drop your retro sticky note exports or notes into a folder. Cowork clusters by theme, prioritizes by impact, produces three specific process improvements.

KPI Dashboard Narratives

Point Cowork at your metrics file. Ask it to write the monthly narrative — the story the numbers are telling — ready to paste above your dashboard.

Issue Log Triage

Drop your issue log into a folder. Cowork categorizes by severity, suggests owners, and drafts a resolution framework for your top five open items.

Power Feature — Automation

Scheduled Tasks: Cowork While You Sleep

Most people use Cowork reactively — they open it, assign a task, wait. But Cowork's scheduler turns it into something closer to a part-time team member. Type /schedule in any task and set it to run daily, weekly, or at sprint end. Your desktop must be on and the app must be open, but that's the only constraint.

For a PM, this is where the compounding starts. The tasks you hate most — weekly status reports, risk log refreshes, issue triage summaries — are exactly the tasks that run well on a schedule.

High-Value PM Scheduled Tasks

Every Friday · 4:00 PM
Weekly Status Report

Reads your weekly notes file, generates the executive status report, saves it as a dated .docx. You review and send. Takes you from 45 minutes to 5 minutes.

Every Monday · 8:00 AM
Meeting Minutes Digest

Reads any new transcript files dropped into your meetings folder over the weekend. Extracts action items, decisions, and open questions. Delivers a single consolidated digest.

Every Sprint End
Risk Log Refresh

Reads the latest sprint notes and issue log. Updates risk likelihood scores, flags any new risks that emerged, and flags stale mitigations that need reviewing.

Every Fortnight
Stakeholder Comms Audit

Checks your sent communications folder and flags any stakeholder who hasn't received an update in over two weeks. Prompts you with a suggested catch-up message.

Every Month · 1st
Budget Tracker Update

Reads your actuals folder, compares against the budget framework, flags any categories running over 80% of allocation, and produces a one-page spend summary.

Every Quarter
Lessons Learned Snapshot

Synthesizes all retro notes and issue logs from the past quarter into a rolling lessons learned document. Builds your institutional memory automatically.

How to Set Up a Scheduled Task

In any Cowork task, type /schedule and describe the frequency: "Run this every Friday at 4pm." Cowork will confirm the schedule and show it in your sidebar under Scheduled. You can view, edit, pause, or delete scheduled tasks from there at any time. The desktop app must be running when the task is due.

Usage Note

Scheduled tasks consume your Cowork usage allowance each time they run. Audit your scheduled tasks monthly and remove any that are no longer producing value. On Pro plans especially, reserve scheduling for your highest-ROI recurring tasks.

Phase 06 — Risk & Issues

Thinking Around Corners

Risk management done well is a competitive advantage. Cowork helps you think more systematically — and because it can read your existing project files, the output is grounded in your actual context, not generic advice.

6.1 Pre-mortem Facilitation

Cowork Task — Pre-mortem

Read all documents in [project folder]. It is 12 months from now and this project has failed. Write a post-mortem from that future, describing the three most likely causes of failure in specific, realistic detail based on what's in these files. Then reverse-engineer: what should we do right now to prevent each one? Save as PreMortem_[project].docx.

6.2 Decision Analysis

Weighted Decision Matrix

Give Cowork your options and evaluation criteria. Ask for a weighted matrix in Excel and flag which criteria are most sensitive to weight changes.

Devil's Advocate Mode

Tell Cowork your preferred option and ask it to argue against it as forcefully as possible. Best stress test before you commit.

Second-Order Effects

"If we make this decision, what are the second and third-order consequences we're likely not thinking about?"

Reversibility Test

Ask Cowork to classify each option by reversibility and cost of being wrong. One-way doors deserve far more scrutiny than two-way ones.

6.3 Issue Escalation Triage

Cowork Task — Issue Triage

Read the issue log in [folder]. For each open issue: severity level, resolution owner, whether it needs steering committee escalation, and recommended next action within 48 hours. Output as an updated issue log with a triage summary at the top.

Practical Skills — Correction

When Cowork Gets It Wrong

Cowork will occasionally produce outputs that miss the mark — wrong tone, wrong structure, wrong level of detail. This isn't a failure; it's a feedback loop. The key is knowing which correction approach fits the situation, so you don't waste time restarting when a nudge would do, or over-iterating when a clean restart is faster.

Use When: Minor Miss
Nudge In-Task

Reply in the same Cowork task with a specific correction: "The tone is too formal — rewrite the executive summary as if briefing a trusted colleague, not a board." Keep the context alive and iterate from the existing output.

Use When: Wrong Structure
Restructure the Brief

If the output structure is wrong, don't patch it sentence by sentence. Go back to your task description, add explicit structure instructions ("Section 1: …, Section 2: …"), and rerun. One clear brief beats three rounds of patching.

Use When: Fundamentally Off
Start a Clean Task

If the output is fundamentally wrong — wrong audience, wrong scope, wrong file — stop iterating. Delete the task, write a new brief using the four-element framework from the Prompt Craft chapter, and rerun fresh. Clean starts beat long correction chains every time.

The Correction Checklist

Before deciding which correction approach to use, run through these four questions:

  • Is the content directionally right, just imprecise? → Nudge in-task with a specific one-sentence correction.
  • Is the structure wrong but the source material fine? → Add explicit structure to your brief and rerun.
  • Did Cowork use the wrong files or wrong context? → Check your folder permissions and file naming, then restart.
  • Is the output confidently wrong on a factual claim? → Never iterate on a factual error. Correct the fact manually, flag it in your global instructions if it's a recurring mistake, and treat all factual claims as needing verification regardless of how confident the output sounds.
Best Prevention

Most correction cycles are caused by under-specified tasks, not by Cowork errors. If you're correcting the same type of output repeatedly, the fix is upstream — in your Global Instructions or in your task template — not in the individual correction.

Phase 08 — Team Management

The People Side of the Work

Project management is fundamentally people management. Cowork won't replace your emotional intelligence — but it removes the documentation overhead so you can spend more time on the relationship side.

8.1 Capacity Planning

Cowork Task — Capacity Allocation

Read the team roster in [team-profiles.docx] and the sprint plan in [sprint-backlog.xlsx]. Suggest an allocation plan that balances workload across the team, avoids single-point-of-failure risk, and flags any skill gaps needing hiring or training. Save as CapacityPlan_Sprint[N].docx.

8.2 Team Development

30-60-90 Onboarding Plans

Describe the new team member's role. Cowork generates a tailored onboarding plan with key contacts, documents to read, and quick wins per period — saved as a doc.

1:1 Question Sets

Claude generates 10 tailored questions — different sets for a high performer needing stretch vs. someone showing early disengagement signals.

Team Health Diagnostics

Describe current dynamics. Cowork suggests what to ask in 1:1s, warning signals to watch, and interventions that typically work for the pattern you're describing.

Recognition Messaging

Describe a specific contribution. Cowork writes recognition that is concrete and genuine — calibrated for team shoutout or formal commendation.

Power Feature — Mobile

Claude in Meetings, in Real Time

Pairing the Claude mobile app with your desktop Cowork session unlocks a workflow most PMs overlook entirely. You can assign tasks to Cowork from your phone, monitor progress remotely, and use Claude on mobile to capture and process live meeting content — all feeding back into your desktop project folder.

The Mobile + Desktop Workflow for PMs

1

Pair your mobile and desktop apps

Install the Claude mobile app and sign in with the same account. In Settings, pair the apps. Your desktop needs to be running and awake for Cowork tasks assigned from mobile to execute.

Both apps must be at their latest versions to pair successfully.
2

Use mobile Claude to capture during meetings

In a live meeting, use Claude on your phone to take structured notes: decisions, action items, open questions. You're not transcribing — you're curating. Paste the output into a notes file in your project folder after the meeting.

3

Assign the Cowork task from your phone, mid-commute

On your way back from a client meeting, open the Claude mobile app and assign a Cowork task: "Read the notes I just dropped in /Q2-Launch/meetings/ and produce a formal meeting minutes document." Your desktop picks it up and executes. By the time you're back at your desk, it's done.

Cowork tasks assigned from mobile execute on your desktop — your computer must stay awake.
4

Use Claude in Chrome for real-time web research

Enable Claude in Chrome as a Cowork connector. During stakeholder prep, ask Cowork to research a stakeholder's organization, recent news, or industry context and compile a one-page brief. It navigates your browser, pulls the information, and saves it — without you switching windows.

Pre-Meeting Brief on Demand

Before a stakeholder call, assign a Cowork task from mobile: "Produce a one-page brief on [organization] covering recent news, key initiatives, and likely concerns for a project conversation." Ready when you open your laptop.

Post-Meeting Action Dispatch

Right after a meeting ends, voice-note your action items into your phone's Claude app. Ask it to format them. Drop the file in your folder. Cowork converts them into a structured action log and updates your issue tracker.

Travel Status Updates

Assign your Friday status report from your phone on the commute home. Cowork reads the week's notes on your desktop and has the report ready before you walk through the door.

Real-Time Risk Flagging

If something goes wrong mid-meeting, voice a quick note into Claude mobile. Ask it to draft an escalation memo and drop it in your escalations folder for Cowork to format and save when you get back.

Privacy Note

Be thoughtful about what meeting content you capture through Claude, especially in client or legally sensitive contexts. Check your organization's AI usage policy before processing any confidential or regulated meeting content through Cowork.

Phase 09 — Project Closeout

Closing With Credibility

Closeout is chronically rushed. Cowork can read across your entire project folder — risk log, issue log, retro notes, status reports — and synthesize a complete closure pack in one task.

Cowork Task — Full Closure Pack

Read all documents in [project folder]. Produce a project closure pack containing: (1) executive summary (one page), (2) benefits realization report comparing planned vs. actual outcomes, (3) lessons learned report structured by project phase, (4) five recommendations for future projects of this type, (5) knowledge transfer summary for the receiving team. Save as ClosurePack_[project].docx.

Lessons Learned Report

Point Cowork at your full project folder. It reads retro notes, risk logs, and issue logs, synthesizing them into a structured report with phase-by-phase recommendations.

Benefits Realization Report

Give Cowork the original charter and final delivery status. It drafts a narrative honest about gaps and specific about wins.

Knowledge Transfer Docs

Describe what you're handing over. Cowork generates a transfer document structure calibrated for the recipient team's technical level.

Close-out Presentation

Ask Cowork to produce a 10-slide close-out deck: what we set out to do, what we achieved, what we learned, what comes next.

Final Word

What Cowork Cannot Do For You

This guide has focused on where Cowork adds value. It's equally important to know the ceiling — clearly and without illusion.

  • Cowork doesn't know your organizational politics, stakeholder history, or cultural context. That's your most valuable PM asset — protect and develop it.
  • Cowork's risk identification is pattern-based, not project-specific. It will miss the idiosyncratic risk only you can see from inside the room.
  • Cowork generates plausible-sounding content. Always verify facts, numbers, and technical claims before any output appears in official documents.
  • Cowork can draft a difficult conversation, but it cannot read the room. Delivery, timing, and relationship management remain entirely human.
  • Your desktop app must be open and your computer must be awake for Cowork to run. Background tasks pause if the machine sleeps.
  • Cowork uses more usage allowance than standard chat. For simple one-off questions, regular Claude chat is the right tool.
  • Legal, financial, and HR documents produced by Cowork must go through appropriate review before use in any binding context.
  • Cowork reflects the quality of the inputs you give it. Well-organized project folders produce better outputs than scattered files with inconsistent naming.

The PMs who compound their advantage fastest aren't the ones using AI the most. They're the ones who know exactly when to stop delegating to the machine — and trust their own judgment.
